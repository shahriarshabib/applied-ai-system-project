# Common Behavior Issues and Care Adjustments

## Lethargy and Low Energy

A pet that is sleeping more than usual, slow to greet you, or uninterested in normal activities may be unwell. Causes range from minor (a hot day, recent strenuous exercise) to serious (infection, organ disease, pain).

If lethargy lasts more than 24 hours, or is combined with appetite loss, vomiting, diarrhea, or fever, schedule a veterinary visit. Do not increase exercise to "snap them out of it" — a sick pet needs rest.

In PawPal+, mark a vet appointment as HIGH priority with a fixed time, and consider downgrading walk priority to LOW until the pet is evaluated.

## Excessive Barking or Vocalization

Most barking has a cause: insufficient exercise, separation anxiety, fear, attention-seeking, or alerting to perceived threats. The fix depends on the cause.

For under-exercised dogs, increase walk duration or add a midday play session. For separation anxiety, build up alone-time tolerance gradually and consider a vet consultation about anti-anxiety options. For attention-seeking barking, do not reward it with attention — wait for quiet, then reward.

Cats that vocalize excessively at night are often bored or hungry. A late-evening play session followed by a small meal often helps.

## Destructive Chewing or Scratching

Puppies and kittens explore with their mouths and claws. Provide appropriate outlets (chew toys for dogs, scratching posts for cats) and redirect them when they target inappropriate items. Punishment after the fact does not work — pets cannot connect the punishment to the past behavior.

For adult dogs that suddenly start chewing destructively, the cause is usually under-exercise, anxiety, or boredom. Increasing daily exercise and adding puzzle toys often resolves it within a week.

## House Soiling

In an otherwise house-trained pet, sudden accidents may indicate a medical problem (urinary tract infection, diabetes, kidney issues) and warrant a vet visit. They may also indicate stress from a household change (new pet, new baby, moved furniture, new schedule).

Increase bathroom break frequency, and clean accidents thoroughly with an enzymatic cleaner so the pet does not return to the same spot.

## Aggression

Aggression is a serious behavior problem that often requires professional help. Do not attempt to "dominate" an aggressive pet — this typically makes it worse. A veterinary behaviorist or certified positive-reinforcement trainer is the right resource.

PawPal+ should not attempt to advise on aggression beyond suggesting professional help.

## Anxiety and Fear

Signs include trembling, hiding, panting (in cats), excessive licking, destructive behavior when alone, or refusing to eat. Common triggers are thunderstorms, fireworks, vet visits, and being left alone.

Short-term help: provide a safe quiet space, use calming pheromone diffusers, or play soft music. Long-term: gradual desensitization training and possibly anti-anxiety medications prescribed by a vet.

## Adjusting the PawPal+ Schedule for Behavior Issues

When the user reports a behavior issue, the AI advisor should:
1. Retrieve relevant knowledge from this document and the emergency document if symptoms suggest illness
2. Suggest specific, actionable schedule changes (e.g., "add a 15-minute training task at midday")
3. Recommend a vet visit if the issue could have a medical cause
4. Avoid blaming the owner — frame suggestions as adjustments, not corrections
